The Chicken Or The Egg?

Women want love to be a novel, men a short story. ~Daphne du Maurier

Stories are born in one of two ways, neither of which is the right or the wrong way.

  1. Some stories come to you first as an idea for a plot, but without characters. The goal is then to populate the idea with believable characters. Notice we do not say 'real' characters.

  2. Some stories come to you first as an interesting character, but without a story. The goal is then to explore who this character is and find out what his or her story might be.

Some writers divide authors into these two categories: 'plot-driven' authors, such as Stephen King (Carrie, It) or James Patterson (Alex Cross novels), and 'character-driven' authors, such as Janet Fitch (White Oleander) or Toni Morrison (Beloved).

It does not matter how the story is born and shaped, as long as it becomes a story. However, for this exercise and just for fun, we will start with...

Characters

One of the most difficult things to get right in writing is to make your characters believable. That means that they should seem recognisably human in the ways in which they act, think and speak – unless they are aliens, of course.

  1. Characters drive the storyline.

  2. Characters act and speak differently.

  3. Characters do things for specific reasons.

In order to get this right, you need to know your character extremely well, which simply means that you need to explore, think about and decide on every aspect of that character’s life. This involves their personality, hopes, dreams, and fears.

When writing a short story, your time and space is limited, though. This means you need to be selective. Not every aspect you have thought about will eventually make it onto the page, but the more aspects you think about, the more alive the character will be in your imagination.

His or her environment defines a character and the environment will, in turn define the character. Keep this in mind when you create him or her.

Exercise 7 – The character

Go back to your newspaper story title or use any scene you have written for any of the exercises.

  1. Who is the protagonist in this situation? To whom does everything happen? If it is not clear, pick someone as your main character who might be centrally involved in the playing out of the events described in the story, and work with him or her.

  2. If he or she does not have a name, pick one for him or her. You can change the name if you do not like the original. Write the name on top of a blank page.

  3. Now draw a large stick figure on the page. Make notes of the character’s physique, from hair colour to shoe size.

  4. Answer the following seven questions about him or her: (Write a short paragraph on each question. Be as fresh, quirky, original, and interesting as you can be. Do not pick the obvious or the ordinary. Your character must be unique and fresh. Push yourself. Think.)

    1. Who were his or her childhood friends?

    2. Did he or she have a favourite pet? What was it? Give it a name.

    3. He loved his father or mother more because…

    4. The character wanted a ………………….. for his or her birthday when he or she was 12. Did he or she get it?

    5. He or she wished his or her name was …………………………….

    6. He or she wanted to be a ………………when he or she grew up.

    7. What is the one secret this character would never tell anyone?

Exercise 8 – The other character

Now pick another character.

This should be someone already in the scene or if your character is alone try to create another character.

Do the same process with this character – from the physical to the psychological. Do not worry about how he or she will be involved in the story just yet.

You may give your imagination free reign. Again, make original choices, and – if you can – try to create as big a contrast as possible with your protagonist.

This character will become your opposition character.
