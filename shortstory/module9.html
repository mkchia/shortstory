Definitions:

First Person

  • I, me, my

  • Should be both a character in, and narrator of, the story

  • Can observe all the characters but can only reveal what he is thinking or feeling himself.

Second Person

  • You

  • Use only for surreal effect in exceptional cases

  • May also be the voice of blame

Third Person, Attached (or Free Indirect Discourse)

  • He, him, his

  • Follow a single character in the story, usually the protagonist

  • The narrator tells only what this character sees and feels, and cannot see into other character’s heads (do not head-hop!)

Third Person, Limited

  • He, him, his

  • Simply a narrator

  • Not part of the story and cannot read anyone’s mind

  • Think of a newspaper article

Third Person, Omniscient

  • He, him , his

  • The “God-narrator”

  • Narrator/Author knows all and sees all and often intrudes on the story with own opinions and judgements

In a short story, it is best to choose one viewpoint, and one viewpoint character (usually, the protagonist) and stick to it throughout. The space simply does not allow for multiple viewpoints of multiple viewpoint characters.

Tenses

We write most stories in the simple past tense. (He walked. She gasped. We laughed.) It is the most commonly used of all the tenses in fiction because it works well.

However, we suggest you also try the present tense. It is immediately involving and enables you to manipulate the reader into feeling and experiencing the story in real-time.

The key, again, is consistency.
