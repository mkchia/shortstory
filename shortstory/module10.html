Short fiction seems more targeted - hand grenades of ideas, if you will. When they work, they hit, they explode, and you never forget them. Long fiction feels more like atmosphere: it’s a lot smokier and less defined. ~Paolo Bacigalupi

Your opening scene must immediately intrigue the reader to want to know more. The beginnings must:

  1. Catch the reader’s attention through action

  2. Draw the reader into your character’s world, establish the tone and

  3. Contain the inciting moment.

The idea is to create enough excitement so that the reader wants to read on.

Exercise 17 – The beginning

The opening scene

With the above in mind, write the opening scene of your short story. Choose one of the story ideas you came up with earlier or try a new idea.

Launch it with action. What happens? Is it comical? Is it serious?

  1. Create drama. Be as busy and descriptive as possible. Use colours, noise, action and particularly, dialogue. Do not use flashbacks here. Forget about back-story. Show, show, show as if it is happening in front of you on a big screen.

  2. Do not introduce the antagonist in the scene yet. Let the conflict grow naturally from the events you dramatize, that is, from surrounding smaller characters.

  3. Do not give us the protagonist’s decision or goal yet. He has no time to think or decide. He acts.

  4. Don’t worry too much about the rules right now. This is the first draft. Get the story on the paper.

  5. Once you are done try to identify: the goal, conflict and disaster.
