Somewhere around the place I’ve got an unfinished short story about Schrodinger’s Dog; it was mostly moaning about all the attention the cat was getting. ~Terry Pratchett

The true writer’s philosophy:

Do not trust the muse. He or she is too flighty and temperamental.

Rather open your eyes and ears to what happens around you. Ideas are everywhere. Switch on that automatic recorder in the back of your head. Stories are around us, every day, everywhere. The art lies in recognising and remembering them as little sparks for creative exploration.

  1. Personal experience. Have you witnessed, experienced, or read about, a dramatic event? Have you had an encounter with an intriguing person? Were you jilted? Has something or somebody repulsed you? Did someone sell you a used car that you had to return? Was your pet killed in a car accident? How did that make you feel? What did you do?

  2. Social issues. Are there social issues that anger or intrigue you? Here are some examples:

    • Men who are paid more than women to do the same job.
    • The government’s lack of concern about the safety of its citizens.
    • Unfair laws.

    How would you dramatize an incident relating to this issue? Which kind of characters would experience these issues first-hand?

  3. What if… Play a game and imagine what could wrong, or right, for every scenario of your day. Think in opposites, swop gender roles, think of the worst thing that could happen and then of the best thing that could happen. Imagine a giant octopus climbing out of the ocean. Imagine your boss running through boss butt naked after he gets fired. Have fun and then write it down.

Think about themes. Examples: Work versus home; a woman in a man’s world; the more things change the more they stay the same.

Three Golden Rules of Writing:

  1. Write Every Day. Set up a writing routine and stick to it. Writing is a job. Make deadlines and stick to them.

  2. Write What You Love To Read. There is no sense in writing a luscious love story if you do not believe in it yourself. Always try to imagine that someone else has written this story – would you pick it up and read it? Or would you ignore it from the beginning?

  3. Write Because You Have No Choice. It is all good and well to want to win prizes, but accolades and money are never the best driving forces behind good writing. Writing is a craft; it takes time, patience and practise, often for very little actual reward. If this deters you from wanting to do it, stop now.

Exercise 6 - Ideas

  1. Personal Experience: Write a scene about something that happened to you or that you saw. It can be funny, sad, scary. Anything you can thing of.

  2. Social Issues:

    a) Read news stories in popular newspapers or websites. Pick one that interests or intrigues you the most, or one that deals with an issue you feel strongly about. React impulsively or viscerally. Do not think about why you’ve chosen it too much. Just pick one.

    b) Circle a single keyword in the story you have chosen (not a name or a place) that occurs most, or that summarises the gist of the story for you, or that tickles your imagination. Again, react viscerally. Do not think too much.

    c) Brainstorm! Spider-Chart! Colours! Connections!

    d)Come up with a title for a story from your brainstorm: not a title for a newspaper article, but a title for a short story. Something that would make a potential reader stop and wonder what this is about. Be quirky. Be original. Have fun. Nothing is too weird or too strange.

    e) Write the story. Don’t worry about what it should or shouldn’t be or what the rules are. Just get it down so that you have something to edit later.

  3. What if… a. Write down three things that you did today. Bought groceries, typed an email etc. b. Now think in opposites or of anything crazy. What is weirdest, worst, funniest thing could happen? c. Write the scene.
