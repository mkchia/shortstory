Direct speech, or dialogue, will help you solve the show-versus-tell problem, because dialogue dramatizes situations.

Dialogue is a tool. Use it to increase pace, to describe personalities, to show their reactions and actions, and to colour your story.

Check that your dialogue achieves one of the following:

  1. Reveals something about the character.

  2. Shows something about another character.

  3. Describes the situation.

  4. Gives necessary background information.

  5. Sets a mood or tone.

  6. Leads to the next step in the story.

  7. Adds another element to the plot.

What is a dialogue tag?

Dialogue tags tell us when a character is speaking. They are every ‘he said’ and ‘she said’ in the books you read. Use them. Readers do not like to be confused and you do not want them to lose interest and stop reading. Do not add adverbs to these tags.

Tip: When there are only two characters in a scene, place the dialogue tags for each one in the first natural breaks after they speak. You can choose to describe actions, thoughts, and expressions without the tags after you’ve done this – as long as there is no confusion about who is speaking. However, always use the tags when there are three or more characters involved.
