Before you start writing, it is a good idea to plan your story. All good stories have a beginning, a middle, and an end. The conventional plot plan dictates that you have an opening scene, which contains the inciting moment. This moment is inciting because it sparks the action of the story and it sets things in motion.

The inciting moment also causes the protagonist to make a decision or set a goal. He embarks on a new path, which will eventually lead him to change, whether he realises this or not.

Of course, a certain character might want to oppose this path of change in the main character for various reasons. This character is called the antagonist. Who is the other character you created in Exercise 8?

He or she is the key source of conflict in the storyline. The antagonist could be anybody from a jealous spouse, a false friend, a worried doctor, an angry boss, a police officer, to an overprotective parent. This should be a person who has a good reason to want the protagonist to stay the same.

No story can exist without conflict, and conflict comes from opposing characters.

Tip: Antagonists are not necessarily evil. In fact, and in order to create believable antagonists, their motivations must be just as valid and reasonable in objecting to or resisting the main character’s new path of action
